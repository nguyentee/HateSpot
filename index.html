5 Years of

Police-Reported Hate Crimes


in Ottawa


From 2015 to 2020


The data we’re working with are the raw numbers of hate and bias-motivated incidents that were reported to the Ottawa Police. A police-reported hate crime is


“defined as a criminal violation against a person or property motivated by hate, based on race, national or ethnic origin, language, colour, religion, sex, age, mental or physical disability, sexual orientation or gender identity or expression, or any other similar factor.”


The crime statistics are published and accurate on the day that they were produced. The details include year, reported date & time, occurrence date, time & weekday, hate crime type, primary hate crime motivation, primary offence, CCJS clearance, location, Ottawa neighbourhood, City, and Ottawa police sector & division.


Motivation


The number of hate and bias-motivated incidents have increased over the last few years with cases continuing to rise at rapid rates. The interactive map and bar graph below highlight the different police-reported hate and bias crime rates in various Ottawa neighbourhoods. The visualizations allow users to select a general location and/or input an address, and filter it by selecting a specific time period (years) and type of hate and bias-motivated incident. The visualizations and data presented on this page were created for policy-makers, police, educators, and other stakeholders that may be in a position to raise awareness and take the necessary steps to implement positive change. This data is also beneficial to current and potential Ottawa residents who may or may not be a part of targeted groups in order to bring awareness of what is happening within their communities, and to advocate and support one another.

Design Process


Our first step was to decide what data we wanted to focus on from our dataset. We decided to focus on location, hate crime type, motivation, and year. One of our main focuses is on the location and we want to have a map that interacts with another graph that shows the specific data. We developed our own individual sketches using pencil and paper that we thought would best represent the data in a visualization. Afterwards, we met as a group and voted for the single best design that best represented our desired data.


The following three are our top chosen designs:



We decided to create a stacked bar chart as our main graph. After deciding on our favourite sketch, we then developed higher fidelity designs using Tableau based on our original sketch. We all made our own different designs and then decided on the best design as a group. We made sure to follow basic design principles while we were developing our designs. While creating our designs, we made sure our data was accurately represented and there was no outstanding lie factor. We avoided using 3D models as there seemed to be no need for it and would just make our graph more confusing. We made sure the different data sections could be clearly identified using labels. We focused on being able to separate sections with lightness and darkness so it could be visible for people that are RGB colourblind. The final picked design is the same visualization we are using for our website now. The development process for our final copy was slow but was very important to ensure we do not bump into any major errors later in the project.


Final Visualization


For our final visualization, we created an interactive dashboard using a map and a bar graph of Ottawa neighbourhoods. All of our code is hosted on Github and has been downloaded into a zip file. Both of the visualizations were created on Tableau and we used brushing and linking for both visualizations to interact with one another; when you click on a location of the map, the bar graph filters with the map location(s) and displays other information linked. And when you click on any section of the bar graph, the map filters to the location(s) that correlate with the bar graph. To make the visualization work on our webpage, we had to embed the code into our index.html file.


Walkthrough


When you (the user) first click on our webpage, you are informed about the dataset we used and the interactive visualizations we created. When you scroll down, the map and bar graph of Ottawa neighbourhoods are present. On the right side of the visualization, you are presented with different legends. The first legend is of the hate crime types on Ottawa neighbourhoods, consisting of the categories: Age, Language, Mental or Physical Disability, Race/Ethnicity, Religion, Sex, and Sexual Orientation. The next legend is a filter for the bar graph, where you can scroll from left to right or right to left if you want to focus on one year or multiple at once. The last legend is a colour map, which correlates with the bar graph showing dark blue to a lighter blue, distinguishing the colours so you can identify that the dark blue is the year 2015 and light blue is the year 2020. To use this visualization, you can click on any location on the map (the circles) to uncover the details-on-demand: Hate Crime Type, Location, Hate Crime Motivation, and Year. Once clicked on a location, the bar graph will filter to the bar where this hate crime occurred.



To unclick the location, simply click anywhere on the map where it does not have a circle. This will bring everything back to the beginning. You can do this same action on the bar graph by clicking one area on the bar. Once you click on a bar, the map filters to the year it occurred and the type of hate crime it was. Again, to unclick the location, simply click any white space on the bar graph and this will bring everything back to normal. To use the “Hate Crime Type” legend, click on any of the filters. For example, by clicking, “Age”, the map and bar graph filter to the “Age” hate crimes.



In this instance, the map shows two age-related hate crimes, and the bar graph filtered to the “Children” and the “Unknown Age” sections on the x-axis. To unclick the hate crime type, simply click “Age” for the second time. This will bring everything back to normal.



Data Analysis


Each year from 2015-2019, the number of police-reported hate crime incidents has rather steadily risen, but it jumps sharply/dramatically in number in 2020. From 2015-2019, the average rate of increase in reported hate crimes each year was 14%. In 2020, the rate jumped to 54% from 2019.



From 2015-2019, Jewish people were the most targeted group, until 2020 where Black people were the most targeted.


- Hate crimes against Jewish people made up 66% of all religious-based hate crimes from 2015-2020


- Hate crimes against Black people made up almost 50% of all racial/ethnic-based hate crimes from 2015-2020


- Hate crimes against Black people made up 56% of all racial/ethnic-based hate crimes in 2020


- From 2015-2019, the average rate of increase in Black hate crimes each year was 27%. In 2020, the rate jumped to 168% from the year prior.


- In 2019, the number of reported hate crimes incidents against Jewish people was 32 and the number of reported cases against Black people was 22



There were 0 reported hate crime cases against East and Southeast Asians in 2015 and 2016


- From 2017-2019, the average rate of increase in East and Southeast Asian hate crimes each year was 27%. In 2020, the rate jumped to 250% from the year prior.


- In total, hate crimes against East and Southeast Asians made up 8% of all racial/ethnic-based hate crimes


- In 2020, hate crimes against East and Southeast Asians made up 13% of all racial/ethnic-based hate crimes of that year




The number of religious-based hate crimes has largely stayed consistent throughout 2015-2020


- The average rate of increase for religious-based hate crimes each year was 9%


- The lowest was in 2015 with 48 reported cases and the highest was in 2019 with 60



From 2015-2019, the number of racial/ethnic-based hate crimes rose steadily and remained consistent, until in 2020 where the number rose sharply from 2019.



From 2015-2019, the average rate of increase for racial/ethnic-based hate crimes each year was 24%. In 2020, the rate jumped to 172% from the year prior.


In 2020, the number of racial/ethnic based hate crimes almost tripled going up to 106 compared to the previous year of 39
